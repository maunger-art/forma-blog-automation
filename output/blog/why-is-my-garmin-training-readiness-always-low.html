Your Garmin shows another red training readiness score. Yesterday was red. Last week was mostly red. You're sleeping reasonably well, following your training plan, yet your device insists you're not ready to train hard. This pattern frustrates serious athletes because it seems disconnected from how you actually feel.

The issue lies in understanding what training readiness actually measures—and why your body might be sending signals that contradict your perceived energy levels. Training readiness algorithms rely heavily on heart rate variability, a metric that reflects your autonomic nervous system's balance between stress and recovery states. When this system tilts toward chronic stress activation, readiness scores plummet regardless of motivation or subjective feelings.

Most athletes experience persistently low readiness due to three primary factors: accumulated training stress that exceeds recovery capacity, lifestyle factors that prevent full parasympathetic activation during rest, or measurement and interpretation errors that create false signals. Understanding these mechanisms—and how to address them—transforms readiness from a frustrating mystery into actionable recovery data.

Why is my Garmin training readiness always low?

Garmin's Training Readiness combines heart rate variability, sleep quality, recovery time, and training load into a single metric. When this score consistently sits in the red zone, it typically signals one of three issues: chronic sympathetic dominance, inadequate recovery protocols, or training load that exceeds your current adaptive capacity.

The algorithm weights HRV as the primary input, measuring beat-to-beat variation in your heart rhythm during sleep. Research shows HRV drops 10-30% when the autonomic nervous system shifts toward sympathetic dominance—your fight-or-flight response. This happens when training stress accumulates faster than your body can process it, when sleep quality deteriorates, or when non-training stressors like work or nutrition create additional physiological burden.

Training Readiness also incorporates recovery time estimates based on exercise intensity and duration. High-intensity sessions above 85% max heart rate can suppress HRV for 24-72 hours, depending on your aerobic fitness level. If you're stacking intense sessions without adequate recovery windows, the algorithm will flag consistently low readiness scores.

Sleep metrics add another layer. Poor sleep efficiency—spending less than 85% of time in bed actually sleeping—reduces HRV recovery by 15-25% compared to optimal sleep. Garmin's sleep tracking identifies these patterns and feeds them directly into your readiness calculation.

How do you train to improve HRV?

Heart rate variability responds predictably to specific training adaptations. The most effective approach centers on aerobic base development through polarized intensity distribution—approximately 80% of training time below aerobic threshold and 20% at high intensity.

Zone 2 training, conducted at 60-75% of maximum heart rate, drives mitochondrial adaptations that improve autonomic function. Studies demonstrate that 6-8 weeks of consistent aerobic base training increases HRV by 12-20% in trained athletes. This occurs because enhanced aerobic capacity reduces the relative stress of daily activities and moderate training loads.

High-intensity work must be strategically periodized to avoid chronic sympathetic activation. Research supports limiting high-intensity sessions to 2-3 per week, with at least 48 hours between sessions above 85% max heart rate. This allows sufficient recovery for HRV to return to baseline levels.

Strength training contributes to HRV improvement through different mechanisms. Compound movements like squats and deadlifts, performed at 70-85% of one-rep max, enhance neuromuscular efficiency and reduce the energetic cost of locomotion. This translates to lower heart rates at submaximal intensities, supporting better autonomic balance.

Training consistency matters more than training volume for HRV adaptation. Athletes who maintain moderate, consistent training loads show 15-25% better HRV scores than those following high-volume, irregular patterns. The autonomic nervous system adapts to predictable stress but struggles with chaotic training schedules.

Why is my HRV so low?

Low HRV typically stems from sympathetic nervous system dominance, where your body remains in a heightened state of alertness instead of shifting into parasympathetic recovery mode. This physiological state can result from training factors, lifestyle stressors, or underlying health issues.

Overreaching represents the most common training-related cause. When training load exceeds recovery capacity for 2-3 weeks, HRV can drop 20-40% below baseline values. This occurs because accumulated metabolic stress—lactate, inflammatory markers, hormonal disruption—keeps the sympathetic system activated even during rest periods.

Sleep disturbances directly suppress HRV through multiple pathways. Sleep debt of just 1-2 hours per night reduces HRV by 8-15% within a week. Poor sleep quality, measured by frequent awakenings or reduced REM sleep, prevents the parasympathetic nervous system from achieving full recovery activation during the night.

Non-training stressors create physiological responses identical to exercise stress. Work deadlines, relationship issues, or financial pressure trigger cortisol release and sympathetic activation. Research shows psychological stress can reduce HRV by 10-25%, independent of training load.

Nutritional factors also influence HRV patterns. Chronic under-fueling, particularly carbohydrate restriction below 3-5g per kg body weight in endurance athletes, elevates stress hormones and suppresses HRV. Dehydration of just 2-3% body weight loss similarly impacts autonomic function. Alcohol consumption reduces HRV for 12-48 hours after intake, depending on the amount consumed.

Why is my readiness score so low?

Readiness scores integrate multiple physiological markers beyond just HRV, creating a more comprehensive but sometimes confusing picture of recovery status. Garmin's algorithm combines HRV, sleep metrics, training load, and recovery time estimates, weighting each factor based on recent patterns in your data.

Sleep quality often drives readiness scores more than athletes expect. The algorithm examines sleep efficiency, REM percentage, and deep sleep duration. If you're achieving less than 20% REM sleep or spending under 15% of the night in deep sleep stages, readiness scores will reflect this deficit even if HRV appears normal.

Training load calculation considers both acute load (last 7 days) and chronic load (last 4 weeks). When acute load exceeds chronic load by 20-30%, the readiness algorithm flags potential overreaching. This explains why readiness can drop even after single hard sessions if they represent significant spikes above your normal training pattern.

Recovery time estimates factor in exercise intensity and individual fitness markers. High-intensity sessions above lactate threshold require 48-72 hours for full physiological recovery in most athletes. If you're training again before this window closes, readiness scores will remain suppressed regardless of how you feel subjectively.

Environmental and lifestyle factors receive less algorithmic weight but still influence scores. Temperature extremes, altitude changes, and travel across time zones can suppress readiness for 3-7 days as your body adapts to new conditions. The algorithm learns these patterns over time and adjusts expectations accordingly.

Is an HRV of 150 good?

HRV values are highly individual and depend entirely on the measurement method used. An HRV reading of 150 milliseconds using RMSSD calculation would be exceptionally high—well above the 95th percentile for most populations. However, different devices and algorithms produce vastly different numerical ranges for the same physiological state.

Garmin devices typically report HRV values between 20-100ms for most users when using RMSSD methodology. Values above 100ms generally indicate excellent autonomic function and strong parasympathetic activity. Elite endurance athletes often display HRV values 40-60% higher than sedentary individuals due to superior aerobic fitness and autonomic adaptation.

Context matters more than absolute numbers. A trained athlete with a typical HRV of 45ms who suddenly jumps to 150ms might actually be experiencing a negative adaptation—potentially indicating illness, dehydration, or measurement error. Consistent day-to-day patterns provide more actionable information than single readings.

Age and gender significantly influence HRV ranges. Males typically show 10-20% higher HRV values than females, while HRV declines approximately 1-2ms per year after age 30. A 150ms reading would be more remarkable in a 50-year-old athlete than a 25-year-old.

Measurement timing and conditions critically affect HRV accuracy. Morning readings taken immediately upon waking, before movement or stress, provide the most reliable data. Readings taken after coffee, during illness, or following poor sleep can vary by 50-100% from true resting values.
