Your Garmin shows another red training readiness score. Yesterday's easy run felt harder than it should have. Your heart rate variability has been declining for weeks, yet you're following your training plan religiously and getting adequate sleep. This scenario frustrates thousands of endurance athletes who invest in recovery metrics but struggle to interpret what their bodies are actually telling them.

The problem lies in a fundamental misunderstanding of how autonomic nervous system metrics work. Most athletes treat HRV and training readiness like a simple fuel gauge—expecting higher numbers to correlate directly with better performance. But these metrics reflect complex interactions between training stress, lifestyle factors, and individual physiological responses that vary dramatically between athletes. A chronically low readiness score often indicates not overtraining, but rather poor autonomic balance or measurement inconsistencies that can be systematically addressed.

Why is my Garmin training readiness always low?

Why is my Garmin training readiness always low?

Your Garmin training readiness stays perpetually low because the algorithm is fundamentally conservative and relies on metrics that penalize consistent training. Training readiness scores below 50 often reflect the device's interpretation of your autonomic nervous system state, not your actual capacity to train effectively.

Garmin's readiness calculation heavily weights heart rate variability trends and sleep quality scores. When your HRV drops even slightly below your rolling baseline, the algorithm assumes you need recovery. This creates a paradox for dedicated athletes: consistent training naturally suppresses HRV through accumulated fatigue, even when you're adapting well to your training load.

The issue compounds because Garmin uses a 7-day rolling average for HRV baseline calculations. If you've been training consistently for weeks, your recent HRV values will always appear suppressed compared to periods of lighter training or rest. Research from Plews et al. (2013) demonstrates that elite endurance athletes often maintain lower HRV during intensive training blocks while still performing optimally.

Three specific factors consistently drive down Garmin readiness scores:

  • Sleep debt accumulation — even 30 minutes less sleep than your average triggers readiness penalties
  • Stress score momentum — consecutive days above 150 training stress score create compounding negative effects
  • HRV coefficient of variation — high day-to-day HRV variability, common in well-trained athletes, confuses the algorithm

Your low readiness scores likely indicate that you're training consistently rather than sporadically. Well-adapted athletes often see readiness scores between 25-45 during productive training phases. The key is distinguishing between algorithmic conservatism and genuine overreaching — something that requires looking beyond the single readiness number to your actual performance metrics and subjective feelings.

How do you train to improve HRV?

How do you train to improve HRV?

Training to improve HRV requires a fundamental shift in how you approach intensity distribution and recovery. The parasympathetic nervous system responds best to consistent aerobic base building rather than frequent high-intensity sessions that elevate sympathetic tone.

Research shows that athletes with 80% of their training volume below ventilatory threshold demonstrate higher resting HRV values compared to those following more polarized approaches. This means spending more time in Zone 1 and easy Zone 2, where your body can maintain parasympathetic dominance during and after exercise.

Controlled breathing practices directly stimulate vagal tone and measurably increase HRV within 4-6 weeks of consistent practice. Box breathing (4-4-4-4 count) or extended exhale patterns (4-6 or 4-8 count) performed for 10-15 minutes daily create the strongest adaptations. The key is regularity, not duration.

Sleep optimization produces the most dramatic HRV improvements. Studies tracking athletes over 12 weeks found that increasing sleep duration from 6.5 to 8 hours per night resulted in 15-25% HRV increases, independent of training load changes. Sleep consistency matters more than total duration — going to bed and waking at identical times every day stabilizes circadian rhythms that govern autonomic balance.

Training stress management becomes critical for sustained HRV gains. This means:

  • Limiting consecutive high-intensity days to prevent sympathetic overreach
  • Using objective HRV data to guide training adjustments rather than following rigid schedules
  • Incorporating active recovery sessions that maintain movement without elevating stress hormones

Cold exposure therapy shows promising results, with regular ice baths or cold showers creating measurable vagal tone improvements within 3-4 weeks. However, timing matters — cold exposure immediately post-workout can blunt training adaptations, so separate these interventions by at least 4 hours from key sessions.

Why is my HRV so low?

Why is my HRV so low?

Low HRV typically indicates that your sympathetic nervous system is dominant, meaning your body is stuck in a stress-response state rather than the recovery-focused parasympathetic mode that promotes adaptation.

The most common culprit is cumulative training stress without adequate recovery. Unlike acute fatigue from a single hard session, chronically low HRV develops when you consistently push training volume or intensity faster than your autonomic nervous system can adapt. Research shows that HRV can remain suppressed for 24-72 hours after high-intensity sessions, but this window extends significantly when sessions are stacked without proper recovery.

Sleep quality and duration directly impact HRV more than most athletes realize. Even moderate sleep debt — getting 6 hours instead of your normal 7-8 — can suppress HRV by 15-20% the following day. Poor sleep architecture, frequent awakenings, or inconsistent sleep schedules compound this effect.

Life stress affects your autonomic nervous system identically to training stress. Your body cannot distinguish between the physiological demands of interval training and work deadlines, relationship conflicts, or financial pressure. High life stress periods require reduced training loads to maintain the same HRV baseline.

Nutrition timing and hydration status also influence HRV readings. Dehydration as mild as 2% body weight loss can reduce HRV, while late meals or alcohol consumption shift autonomic balance toward sympathetic dominance during sleep.

Some athletes have naturally lower HRV due to genetics, training history, or age. A 45-year-old cyclist might have an optimal HRV of 25ms while a 25-year-old runner sits at 45ms. Focus on your personal trends and patterns rather than absolute values. Consistent downward trends over 7-14 days signal the need for intervention, regardless of whether your baseline appears "low" compared to population averages.

Why is my readiness score so low?

Why is my readiness score so low?

Your readiness score reflects more than just your training load. While you may have addressed the technical aspects of HRV measurement and improved your baseline values, a persistently low readiness score often indicates that your autonomic nervous system remains suppressed despite these improvements.

The most common culprit is accumulated training stress without adequate recovery. Research shows that even a 10% increase in weekly training volume can suppress parasympathetic activity for 48-72 hours beyond the expected recovery window. If you are stacking sessions before full autonomic recovery occurs, your readiness scores will remain chronically depressed regardless of your absolute HRV values.

Sleep quality represents another critical factor that HRV improvements alone cannot overcome. Sleep debt accumulates exponentially — missing one hour of sleep per night for a week creates the same autonomic disruption as staying awake for an entire night. Your Garmin tracks sleep duration but cannot measure sleep architecture. Poor REM or deep sleep phases will suppress readiness scores even when total sleep time appears adequate.

External stressors compound the problem. Work deadlines, travel, dietary changes, and relationship stress all activate the same physiological pathways as training stress. Your body cannot distinguish between physical and psychological stressors when calculating autonomic balance. Life stress can override training adaptations and keep readiness scores low despite perfect training execution.

Finally, consider measurement consistency. Readiness algorithms rely on baseline comparisons over rolling 30-day periods. If your measurement timing, hydration status, or sleep position varies significantly, the algorithm interprets this variability as instability rather than improvement. Consistent measurement protocols remain essential even after addressing underlying HRV issues.

Is an HRV of 150 good?

Is an HRV of 150 good?

The absolute value of your HRV score means little without context. An HRV of 150 milliseconds could indicate excellent recovery for one athlete and poor recovery for another. What matters is your individual baseline and recent trends, not arbitrary thresholds or comparisons to other athletes.

HRV varies dramatically between individuals due to factors like age, genetics, training history, and measurement methodology. A 25-year-old cyclist might have a typical resting HRV of 80ms, while a 45-year-old runner might consistently measure 40ms. Both could be perfectly healthy and well-recovered within their individual ranges.

The key insight from HRV research is that relative changes matter more than absolute values. A drop of 20-30% below your seven-day rolling average typically indicates accumulated stress, regardless of whether your baseline is 30ms or 150ms. This is why effective HRV tracking requires weeks of consistent data collection to establish your personal range.

Different devices also produce different scales. Garmin's HRV stress score uses a 0-100 scale derived from your raw HRV data, while apps like HRV4Training display millisecond values directly. A "good" score on one platform may look poor on another, despite representing identical physiological states.

Most importantly, HRV should be interpreted alongside other recovery markers. Sleep quality, subjective wellness, and performance metrics provide crucial context that HRV alone cannot capture. An athlete might show elevated HRV after a rest day but still feel fatigued from poor sleep or life stress.
